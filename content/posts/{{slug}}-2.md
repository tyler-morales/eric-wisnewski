Located about an hour west of Chicago is the city of DeKalb, Illinois.  My familiarity with the city comes from its famous DeKalb Oasis.  The Oasis is home to greats such as multiple claw machines, a perpetually closed Popeyes, and maybe the grossest bathroom in Illinois.  Northern Illinois University is also there, where my friend Mike and I spent a windy afternoon watching Northern Illinois take on MAC rival Ball State.  

Prior to the match-up, Mike and I stopped in neighboring Sycamore, Illinois, while doing research, and I came across a behemoth of a pork tenderloin sandwich.  The Dirty Bird restaurant has a pork tenderloin sandwich that is the size of a lunchroom tray, which is comically put between a single hamburger bun and a few pickles.  While entering the restaurant, I gestured to Mike, a man eating the sandwich we planned on ordering, and asked the fella if it was any good.

Man: “Yea real good, been looking forward to it all day.”

Eric: *polite smile and nod*

Man: “You guys here from the group?”

Eric: “Group? Which group”

Man “The pork tenderloin Facebook group, that’s how I found this place.”

Mike “No, we aren’t in the group.”

Mike and I laughed our way to the NIU game at the idea of a Facebook group where people share pictures of their pork tenderloin sandwiches. Turns out the joke was on us as there are over one-hundred seventy thousand members of the pork tenderloin Facebook group.

![](/images/uploads/IMG_3715-1.jpeg)

**Campus: F**

NIU is not the most popular college in the state of Illinois, so the need for new buildings may not be necessary.  The majority of the buildings on campus were built in the 1950s-60s, probably due to an increase in enrollment because of the GI Bill.  I don’t know, I am just spitballing here.  In essence, the campus of Northern is lifeless and expressionless, which is an accurate portrayal of the type of architecture popular in the 50s and 60s.  Large airplane radio tower buildings make up the iconic skyline of DeKalb, which I believe are dorms for the students.  The place is really just an eyesore.  Neighboring Sycamore seems to breathe more life than its sister city.  Northern has a reputation for being a commuter school, leading to a lack of student involvement and presence on campus.  Surrounding the campus is a litany of strip malls holding chain restaurants, gas stations, and vape shops. Entering town requires a trek through a colorless tundra.  Northern Illinois University's campus is not pretty to look at and is even less enjoyable to explore.

**Stadium: C-**

NIU Convocation Center is the home to NIU basketball.  The building was built in 2002 for $36 million, seating approximately 10,000 people.  According to Northern’s website, it is their single largest capital expenditure. Wow!  

This thing is ugly to look at.  Matching the aesthetic of the university’s airport theme, this building looks like a terminal entrance at O’Hare, or one of those giant oil storage containers.  

![](/images/uploads/IMG_3717.jpeg)

However, looks can be deceiving, as when you enter the actual playing area, I was pleasantly surprised.  Bowl-shaped in design with little to no obstructed views makes the viewing of games easy for all guests.  A track surrounded the court where inflatable games for children to play were stationed, along with tables of local companies advertising their services.  At other points of the track were vendors for snacks and beverages, making it possible to get items without leaving the site of the game.  The opportunity for additional seating was available, as the majority of seats were stowed away in order for people to access the benefits of the track.  I indulged in Husky-themed seltzer, which was advertised as a cherry vodka drink.  Mike said it tasted less like cherry and more like a carbonated red wine.  I agreed, and we took our seats.  For the fair price of $10, Mike and I sat right at half court in row 2, where we were in the background of the ESPN+ broadcast.  I was wearing a suit and tie, with a notepad and pen, while playing my journalist part.  

![](/images/uploads/IMG_1756.jpeg)

Although the stadium is expressionless, the inside provided a great viewing experience with great use of the space that is provided.  The sound system sounded good, the jumbotron was well kept, and the price of the experience provided an affordable thing to do with friends or family.  Well done, NIU.

**Fan Base: D**

The crowd was made up mostly of families with young kids or seniors.  Both of which had more interest in the companies peddling free knick-knacks and the inflatable pop-o-shot than the game itself.  There was no gameday host, but there were activities taking place during timeouts that garnered almost no engagement from the crowd and acted more like white noise in between plays.

Unlike the previously visited Boston College, Northern Illinois did have a student band.  Thumbs up from me, and we are going to move on because they were not memorable.  Students were in attendance for this game, probably due to a lack of activities on the campus itself. Including the brothers from Phi Sigma Kappa who were in attendance in full garb.  But even with approximately 100-150 students in attendance, engagement from them was low and lacked the community that people have seen in other student sections.

By no means did the arena seem empty, but the attentiveness and reactions were not even close to a good grade.  Almost 2,000 people were in attendance for a meaningless game, and it might have been a band practice with a basketball game being played in the background.  

Mike believes that at the time, my criticism of the crowd was harsh, but I hope that as he reads this, he is reminded of how disappointing it really was and that I am right.  Our trip to DeKalb was more memorable for the pork tenderloin sandwich, which was disappointing.  I did genuinely like the stadium for what it was, and I wish that the fans had a little more life to them.  NIU is leaving the MAC after this year for the Mountain West.  I hope this jump to an arguably better conference will result in more attention to the program for the future.  Good luck, Huskies, I’ll always remember…uh.  Thank you, Northern Illinois, and thank you, old guy taking photos of his sandwich, I love you.
